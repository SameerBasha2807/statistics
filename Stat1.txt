Q1. 
Statistics is the branch of mathematics that deals with the collection, analysis, interpretation, presentation, and organization of data. It involves methods and techniques to gather, summarize, and draw conclusions from data, often to make decisions or predictions based on that data.

Q2.Descriptive Statistics:

Definition: Descriptive statistics involves methods for summarizing and organizing data so that it can be easily understood. This includes measures like mean, median, mode, range, and standard deviation, as well as graphical representations like histograms, bar charts, and scatter plots.
Example: If a teacher wants to summarize the exam scores of a class, they might calculate the average score (mean) and display the distribution of scores in a histogram.
Inferential Statistics:

Definition: Inferential statistics involves techniques that allow us to use samples to make generalizations or predictions about a population. It includes hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and regression analysis.
Example: A pharmaceutical company may test a new drug on a sample group of patients and then use inferential statistics to determine if the drug is effective for the entire population.

Q3.

Nominal Data:

Definition: Data that represents categories without a specific order. It is qualitative and consists of labels or names.
Example: Types of fruits (e.g., apples, oranges, bananas).
Ordinal Data:

Definition: Data that represents categories with a specific order, but the intervals between the categories are not meaningful.
Example: Movie ratings (e.g., poor, average, good, excellent).
Interval Data:

Definition: Data that represents ordered categories with meaningful intervals between values, but without a true zero point.
Example: Temperature in Celsius or Fahrenheit.
Ratio Data:

Definition: Data that represents ordered categories with meaningful intervals and a true zero point, allowing for the calculation of ratios.
Example: Height or weight.

A box plot (or box-and-whisker plot) is commonly used to display data in terms of range, as it shows the minimum, first quartile, median, third quartile, and maximum of a dataset, effectively illustrating the spread and central tendency.

Q10.
Mean: The average of a dataset. It gives a central value but can be skewed by outliers.
Median: The middle value when the data is ordered. It is less affected by outliers and provides a better measure of central tendency in skewed distributions.
Mode: The most frequently occurring value. Useful in identifying the most common category in nominal data.
Measures of Variability:

Range: The difference between the maximum and minimum values. It gives a sense of the spread of the data.
Variance: The average of the squared differences from the mean. It quantifies how much the data varies.
Standard Deviation: The square root of the variance. It provides a measure of the average distance from the mean, indicating the dispersion of the dataset.
